SQL vs NoSQL
  • SQL databases are primarily called Relational Databases (RDBMS), whereas NoSQL database are primarily called as non-relational or distributed databases.
  • SQL databases are table based databases whereas NoSQL databases are document based, key-value pairs, graph databases or wide-column stores. This means that SQL databases represent data in form of tables which consists of n number of rows of data whereas NoSQL databases do not have standard schema definitions which it needs to adhere to.
  • SQL databases have predefined schema whereas NoSQL databases have dynamic schema for unstructured data.
  • SQL databases are vertically scalable whereas the NoSQL databases are horizontally scalable. SQL databases are scaled by increasing the horse-power of the hardware (increasing the CPU, RAM, SSD, etc, on a single server). NoSQL databases are scaled by increasing the database's servers in the pool of resources to reduce the load (add few more servers easily in your NoSQL database infrastructure).
  • SQL databases uses SQL (structured query language) for defining and manipulating the data. In NoSQL database, queries are focused on collection of documents. Sometimes it is also called as UnQL (Unstructured Query Language). The syntax of using UnQL varies from database to database.
  • SQL database examples: MySql, Oracle, Sqlite, Postgres and MS-SQL. NoSQL database examples: MongoDB, BigTable, Redis, RavenDb, Cassandra, Hbase, Neo4j and CouchDb
  • For complex queries, SQL databases are good fit for the complex query intensive environment whereas NoSQL databases are not good fit for complex queries. On a high-level, NoSQL donâ€™t have standard interfaces to perform complex queries, and the queries themselves in NoSQL are not as powerful as SQL query language.
  • For the type of data to be stored, SQL databases are not best fit for hierarchical data storage. But, NoSQL database fits better for the hierarchical data storage as it follows the key-value pair way of storing data similar to JSON data. NoSQL database are highly preferred for large data set (i.e for big data). Hbase is an example for this purpose.
  • For high transactional based applications, SQL databases are best fit for heavy duty transactional type applications, as it is more stable and promises the atomicity as well as integrity of the data. While you can use NoSQL for transactions purpose, it is still not comparable and sable enough in high load and for complex transactional applications.
  • For support, excellent support are available for all SQL database from their vendors. There are also lot of independent consultations who can help you with SQL database for a very large scale deployments. For some NoSQL database you still have to rely on community support, and only limited outside experts are available for you to setup and deploy your large scale NoSQL deployments.
  • For properties, SQL databases emphasizes on ACID properties (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation and Durability) whereas the NoSQL database follows the Brewer's CAP theorem (Consistency, Availability and Partition tolerance).
  • For DB types, on a high-level, we can classify SQL databases as either open-source or close-sourced from commercial vendors. NoSQL databases can be classified on the basis of way of storing data as graph databases, key-value store databases, document store databases, column store database and XML databases.

Stereotype Threat

Stereotype threat is a situational predicament in which people are or feel themselves to be at risk of confirming negative stereotypes about their social group. Since its introduction into the academic literature, stereotype threat has become one of the most widely studied topics in the field of social psychology. Stereotype threat has been shown to reduce the performance of individuals who belong to negatively stereotyped groups. If negative stereotypes are present regarding a specific group, group members are likely to become anxious about their performance, which may hinder their ability to perform at their maximum level. For example, stereotype threat can lower the intellectual performance of African-Americans taking the SAT reasoning test used for college entrance in the United States, due to the stereotype that African-Americans are less intelligent than other groups. Importantly, the individual does not need to subscribe to the stereotype for it to be activated. Moreover, the specific mechanism through which anxiety (induced by the activation of the stereotype) decreases performance is by depleting working memory (especially the phonological aspects of the working memory system).

As for academic performance and belonging to the Asian ethnicity, I "should" be positively influenced. Thinking back, I'm just not too sure. I know I'm heavily influenced by expectations, but I don't know if everyone will be affected the same way and in the same impact level as I am. For example, if I expect to have pizza for dinner (going a different direction), my expectation buildup will make me heavily desire a burger for dinner. So even if a pizza, under most circumstances, will grant me equal-level happiness, I will be disappointed at a pizza dinner. Likewise, my expectations as an Asian American will affect my performance in every arena, as well as coming from a lower-middle income family, being a gamer, going to so-and-so school, etc. As an Asian, I am typically expected more of in the academic world, but expected less in the athletic world. With those expectations, do I perform above or below expectations? I think that is the bigger issue.

Because I am expected to have higher standards in academics, I may study harder to ensure those standards are met. Or...I may not, because I don't want to be part of the stereotype. I think that decision/habit depends more on the individual. In sports, maybe I will try harder so I won't perpetuate the negative outlook of my background. Or, maybe it will give me reason to try less? There are a lot of possibilities and factors that play into it.

In the end, stereotype threat or not, I don't think these used should be excuses for poorer performance for any group of people. Every person is unique. Every person is influenced (positively and negatively) by all different kinds of factors. It's what we do with our situation that should lead to the end result of our product.

Pair Programming + Feedback

Pair programming is an agile software development technique in which two programmers work together at one workstation. One, the driver, writes code while the other, the observer, pointer or navigator, reviews each line of code as it is typed in. The two programmers switch roles frequently.

While reviewing, the observer also considers the "strategic" direction of the work, coming up with ideas for improvements and likely future problems to address. This frees the driver to focus all of his or her attention on the "tactical" aspects of completing the current task, using the observer as a safety net and guide.

My limited experience with pair programming at DBC has been incredibly beneficial and englightening. While I initially thought having a partner would slow down my work, I found it to be quite the opposite. Not only have I been completing those challenges more quickly, the final work is much more complete. I've learned so much from my peers, and highly encourage pair programming!

And as someone who doesn't like confrontation, I often won't bring it up to them, even if I know it will help them a lot. Having constant (anonymous) feedback allows me to give my thoughts to others that I think can improve their abilities.

Also, I am constantly trying to improve myself so I always welcome feedback. No one is perfect, especially me, so I want to hear as much as I can from others to make me a better everything. I'll probably get a little hurt, but I get over it quickly, and really try to learn/apply the feedback/criticisms, rebukes, etc. from others. Life is all about learning and growing!

OOP vs FP

Object-oriented programming (OOP) is a programming paradigm that represents the concept of "objects" that have data fields (attributes that describe the object) and associated procedures known as methods. Objects, which are usually instances of classes, are used to interact with one another to design applications and computer programs. C++, Objective-C, Smalltalk, Delphi, Java, Javascript, C#, Perl, Python, Ruby and PHP are examples of object-oriented programming languages.

On the other hand, Functional programming (FP) is a programming paradigm that treats computation as the evaluation of mathematical functions and avoids changing state and mutable data. It is a declarative programming paradigm, which means programming is done with expressions. In functional code, the output value of a function depends only on the arguments that are input to the function, so calling a function f twice with the same value for an argument x will produce the same result f(x) both times.

The biggest difference is that Imperative programming (which OOP is a type of) is about Control flow while Functional programming is about Data flow. Another way to say it is that functional programming only uses expressions while in imperative programming, both expressions and statements are used. For example, in imperative programming, variables and loops are common when handling state. In functional programming, the state is handled via parameter passing, which avoids side-effects and assignments.

Classes

The code you write should have the following qualities:

  • Transparent: The consequences of change should be obvious in the code that is changing in distant code that relies upon it.
  • Reasonable: The cost of any change should be proportional to the benefits in change achieves.
  • Usable: Existing code shold be usable in new and unexpectd contexts.
  • Exemplary: The code itself should encourage those who change it to perpetuate these qualities

Code that is Transparent, Reasonable, Usable, and Exemplary (TRUE) not only meet today's needs but can also be changed to meet the needs of the future. The first step in creating code that is TRUE is to ensure that each class as a single, well-defined responsibility. (above)

In a class-based OO language like Ruby, methods are defined in classes. The classes you create will affect how you think about your application forever. They define a virtual world, one that constrains the imagination of everyone downstream. You are constructing a box that may be difficult to think outside of.

Applications that are easy to change consist of classes that are easy to reuse. Reusable classes are pluggable units of well-defined behavior that have few entanglements. Thus, a class should do the smallest possible useful thing; that is, it should have a single responsibility.

Enumerables

The Enumerable mixin provides collection classes with several traversal and seraching methods, and with the ability to sort. The class must provide a method 'each', which yields successive members of the collection. If Enumberable#max,#min, or #sort is used, the objects in the collection must also implement a meaningful <=> operator, as these methods rely on an ordering between members of the collection.

Since arrays are very popular in Ruby programming, I'll discuss them a bit. Here are some array enumberator methods: collect/!, combination, cycle, delete_if, drop_while, each, each_index, find_index, index, keep_if, map/!, permutation, reject/!, repeated_combination, repeated_permutation, reverse_each, rindex, and select/!.

The map enumerator is particularly useful and one of my favorites.

map { |obj| block } -> array

Examples

(1..4).map { |i| i*i } #=> [1, 4, 9, 16]
(1..4).collect { "cat" } #=> ["cat", "cat", "cat", "cat"]

Gregoric Thinking Style and Fixed vs. Growth Mindset

According to this Gregoric Thinking Style test I have taken, I am mainly a Concrete Random and Abstract Sequential thinker.

As a Concrete Random thinker, I like experimenting to find answers, take risks, use intuition, and solve problems independently. I learn best when able to use trial-and-error approaches, compete with others, and be given the opportunity to work through problems themselves. These are difficult for me: restrictions and limitations, formal reports, routines, re-doing anything once it's done, keeping detailed records, showing how I got an answer, choosing only one answer, and having no option.

As an Abstract Sequential thinker, I like to be heard, analyze situations before making a decision, and applying logic in solving or finding solutions to problems. I learn best when I have access to experts or references, am placed in stimulating environments, and am able to work alone. These are difficult for me: being forced to work with those of differing views, too little time to deal with a subject thoroughly, repeating the same tasks over and over, have lots of specific rules and regulations, "sentimental" thinking, expressing my emotion, being diplomatic when convincing others, and not monopolozing a conversation.

After having gone through almost 3 weeks at DBC, I have seen how my two thinking styles have affected my learning. While the concrete random has mostly been positive for learning HTML/CSS/JS, my abstract sequential has made a few things more difficult. Since the pace at DBC is so fast-paced, I am forced to move on from languages/topics/challenges faster than I would like, and not have the opportunity to completely learn them inside-out. Also, I often ask experts when I am uncertain, and in solo challenges, I am unable to ask friends (other engineers) for help. In order to combat this, I am forcing myself to get adjusted with DBC's pace and to trust in myself/cohorts/guides/system.

I really enjoyed learning Carol Dweck's breakdown of a Fixed vs. Growth Mindset. While I would subscribe more to the growth mindset, I definitely see myself as a fixed mindset in certain areas and at certain times. I also attribute some of those limitations to my desire for comfort. In many instances, I will avoid challenges and give up easily because I'm unwilling to be uncomfortable. What are the differences between relative, absolute, and fixed positioning? #DBCRAFP

First off, all elements start out as static, meaning they don't have any default values but flow naturally onto a page.

With relative positioning, you add in positioning attributes and that would only affect the element/s relative to where it would be if it was static. So if you type in left: 10px, the element would position itself 10px to the left "relative" to where it would have been without that attribute (static).

With absolute positioning, you are able to place an element exactly anywhere on the page. You give it top, right, bottom, left position attributes, and the element will take that position of the page. These values will be relative to the next parent element with relative or absolute positioning.

Lastly, with fixed positioning, the element is positioned relative to the viewport, or the browser window itself. The viewport doesn't change when the window moves, so even if a user scrolls the page, the element will stay in position.
